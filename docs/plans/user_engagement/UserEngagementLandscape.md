# User Engagement Landscape

How sites in the same general space handle contribution, ownership, and community.

## Contribution Models

There are three broad models for how user contributions work:

- **Wiki model**: Anyone can edit any page. Pages compound and improve over time. But original authors may feel their work was defaced. Requires conflict resolution norms and eventually editorial governance.
- **Authored-contribution model** (Atlas Obscura, blogs): Each piece is attributed to its author and doesn't get edited by strangers. Simpler emotionally, but pages don't compound and you risk duplicate or contradictory contributions.
- **Layered model** (Discogs): Structured data (specs, dates, manufacturers) is wiki-editable. Long-form writing is authored and attributed. Anyone can fix a track listing, but reviews belong to their author.

## Comparable Sites

### MusicBrainz

Open music encyclopedia, community-edited structured data. Probably the closest structural analog to Pinbase.

- Contributors are motivated by completionism: "I will be the one who makes the Dischord Records discography perfect."
- Uses an **open edit system** where proposed changes are visible and votable before they go live. Creates accountability without a gatekeeper.
- Contributors build reputation through accepted edits.
- Downside: high friction, only works for people who are already committed.

### Discogs

Started as a database, became a marketplace, but the database contributors are still the soul of it.

- **Separation between data and opinion.** Anyone can correct a release's track listing (wiki-style, reviewed by community), but reviews and collection notes are personal and attributed.
- Contributors see "you've contributed to X releases" on their profile.
- The marketplace gave Discogs an economic engine that funds the encyclopedia.

### Atlas Obscura

Crowd-sourced encyclopedia of interesting places.

- **Editorial curation as a feature, not a bottleneck.** Users submit places and descriptions, but an editorial team polishes them before they go live.
- The result feels authoritative without being sterile.
- Contributors get credited by name.
- Probably the closest _tone_ model to what Pinbase could be, given the museum's involvement. The museum's team could play the editorial role that Atlas Obscura's staff does.

### iNaturalist

Community science platform.

- **Contribution is inherently meaningful because it serves a cause bigger than the site.** People aren't uploading observations to build iNaturalist's database — they're contributing to scientific knowledge. The platform is the tool, not the point.
- Maps well to the museum's mission: contributors aren't writing for Pinbase, they're _preserving pinball history_. That framing matters.

### Fandom/Wikia

Thousands of niche wikis, including pinball ones.

- **Pure wiki works for established fandoms but struggles with quality.** No editorial layer means anyone can write anything.
- Niche wikis often end up with wildly uneven quality — some pages are beautifully detailed, others are stubs or nonsense.
- The lesson for Pinbase: wiki-style openness without _some_ curation tends to produce a site that feels unfinished.

### Wikipedia

The canonical example of community-built knowledge.

- The primary motivator for most sustained Wikipedia editors is **stewardship**: "I am the person who maintains the article on Bally Manufacturing, and that matters."
- Gamification exists (edit counts, barnstar awards, admin status) but reinforces identity rather than driving it. The identity comes first.
- There's an unspoken understanding that certain editors are the stewards of certain articles.
- Quality is maintained through community norms, editorial guidelines, and a thick governance layer. This works at scale but famously drives away casual contributors.

### IPDB

The existing major pinball encyclopedia.

- Has great data but feels frozen in time — no community energy.
- Difficult for contributors to work with, creating pent-up demand among amateur historians who want a better platform.
- Small editorial team reviews every submission. This keeps quality high but limits throughput and contributor autonomy.

### Pinside

The largest pinball community forum and database.

- Community-driven through reputation and social status.
- Lots of participation, but signal-to-noise ratio suffers.
- UX is dated and the culture can be hostile to newcomers.

## Patterns That Work

The sites that feel best — Atlas Obscura, the best parts of Wikipedia, MusicBrainz — share three traits:

1. **Contributing serves a mission, not just the platform.** Pinbase's museum mission gives it this for free.
2. **Contributors are visible and credited.** Not just a username in a log — their name on the thing they wrote.
3. **There's a quality floor.** Someone, whether community or editorial, ensures the site doesn't feel sloppy.

The sites that struggle — Fandom wikis, unmoderated UGC platforms — are missing #3.

## Open Question: Who Is The Editor?

The quality floor requires someone to maintain it. Options:

- The museum director or staff
- A small team of trusted contributors (like Wikipedia admins)
- The community via voting or review (like MusicBrainz)
- Nobody yet — publish everything and figure it out later

Each answer leads to a very different site. This decision should be made before the contribution system launches.
