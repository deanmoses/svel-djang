The floor cabinet is the standard form factor of the pinball machine: a full-sized, free-standing unit roughly six feet long, two feet wide, and five feet tall at the backbox, weighing between 200 and 300 pounds. The player stands at the front, operating flipper buttons mounted on either side of the cabinet, while the playfield tilts away at roughly six and a half degrees. A vertical backbox rises from the rear, housing the scoring display and translite artwork that gives each machine its visual identity.

The basic proportions of the floor cabinet have remained remarkably stable since the 1950s. Playfield dimensions settled around 20.25 by 42 inches — a size dictated as much by the width of doorways and the capacity of delivery trucks as by any design principle. [[tag:widebody]] machines, roughly four inches wider, appeared periodically when designers needed more playfield real estate, but the standard body remained the overwhelming default. The cabinet's height, depth, and leg geometry were optimized for a standing adult player, and the slight forward lean required to see the playfield and reach the flipper buttons became part of the physical vocabulary of playing pinball.

Every major era of pinball — from the [[technologygeneration:electromechanical]] golden age through [[technologygeneration:solid-state]], [[displaytype:dot-matrix]], and [[displaytype:lcd]] — has been built primarily around the floor cabinet. It is the form factor that operators, collectors, and players mean when they say "pinball machine" without further qualification.
