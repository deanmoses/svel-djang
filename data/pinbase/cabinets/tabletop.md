Tabletop pinball machines are miniaturized versions of full-sized games, designed to sit on a table, counter, or other flat surface at a comfortable playing height. They typically range from two to four feet in length and weigh between 30 and 80 pounds — small enough for two people to carry, large enough to preserve meaningful playfield geometry. The backbox is either integrated into the cabinet or significantly reduced in size, and the legs are absent entirely.

The tabletop format appeared early in pinball's history, with manufacturers producing smaller games for locations that couldn't accommodate a full [[cabinet:floor]] model — small shops, private homes, ship galleys, military recreation rooms. [[manufacturer:gottlieb]], [[manufacturer:williams]], and [[manufacturer:bally]] all produced tabletop models at various points, sometimes as dedicated designs and sometimes as scaled-down versions of popular floor games. The format experienced periodic revivals, notably in the home-market push of the late 1970s and early 1980s when manufacturers sought to sell directly to consumers.

Tabletop machines occupy a space between the full arcade experience of a [[cabinet:floor]] cabinet and the toy-like miniaturization of handheld games. At their best, they deliver genuine pinball gameplay — real [[gameplayfeature:flippers]], real steel balls, real solenoid-driven bumpers — in a package that fits in a living room or a dorm. At their worst, they sacrifice too much playfield complexity to the constraints of the smaller format and feel like compromises rather than complete games.
