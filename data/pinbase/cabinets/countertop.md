Countertop machines are the smallest coin-operated amusement format: compact cabinets designed to sit on a bar, lunch counter, or store checkout and earn revenue in spaces where a [[cabinet:floor]] model would never fit. They are typically under two feet long and light enough for one person to move, with a coin mechanism built into the cabinet and a playfield just large enough to support a few scoring features. Many early countertop games predated the [[gameplayfeature:flippers]] entirely, operating as pure [[gameformat:bagatelle]] — plunger, pins, holes, gravity.

The countertop format was critical to the early expansion of the coin-operated amusement industry in the 1930s. Manufacturers like [[manufacturer:gottlieb]], [[manufacturer:genco]], and [[manufacturer:bally]] produced countertop games by the thousands, placing them in drugstores, cigar shops, candy stores, and diners where they earned pennies and nickels from customers waiting for a prescription or a sandwich. The low cost of the machines and the low commitment required from the location owner made countertop games the entry point for an industry that would grow into the arcade era.

As pinball matured and playfield complexity increased through the 1940s and 1950s, the countertop format gradually gave way to the [[cabinet:floor]] cabinet that could accommodate more features, more targets, and eventually more [[gameplayfeature:flippers]]. Countertop games persisted in novelty and trade-stimulator niches, but the mainstream of pinball design moved decisively to the floor. The countertop remains historically significant as the format that first proved coin-operated amusement could sustain a national industry.
