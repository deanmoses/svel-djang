Pitch-and-bat machines are coin-operated baseball games in which a ball is mechanically pitched — rolled, launched, or dropped into play — and the player swings a bat mechanism to strike it onto a scoring playfield. The bat was typically controlled by a pull knob, a lever, or a trigger mounted on the front of the cabinet, and the timing of the swing determined where the ball landed: a well-timed hit might reach the back wall for a home run, while a mistimed swing produced a grounder that rolled into a low-scoring pocket or an out.

[[manufacturer:williams]] was the most prolific manufacturer of pitch-and-bat games, producing titles from the 1950s through the 1970s that combined the physical satisfaction of hitting an actual ball with the scoring and [[gameplayfeature:replay]] mechanics of the coin-operated amusement industry. The playfield behind the bat was typically laid out to represent a baseball diamond, with scoring pockets or channels corresponding to singles, doubles, triples, home runs, and outs. Some models included mechanical fielder figures that moved to catch or deflect the batted ball.

Pitch-and-bat games occupied a niche alongside pinball in the operator's route — they appealed to the sports-bar crowd and to players who preferred the straightforward physical challenge of hitting a ball over the more abstract geometry of a pinball playfield. Like [[gameformat:shuffle]] alleys, they shared the same manufacturing ecosystem and operator networks as pinball, and their gradual disappearance in the late 1970s and 1980s tracked the same industry-wide contraction that reduced the diversity of coin-operated amusements to pinball and video games.
